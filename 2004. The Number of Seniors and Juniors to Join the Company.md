```md
Table: Candidates

+-------------+------+
| Column Name | Type |
+-------------+------+
| employee_id | int  |
| experience  | enum |
| salary      | int  |
+-------------+------+
employee_id is the primary key column for this table.
experience is an enum with one of the values ('Senior', 'Junior').
Each row of this table indicates the id of a candidate, their monthly salary, and their experience.
 

A company wants to hire new employees. The budget of the company for the salaries is $70000. The company's criteria for hiring are:

Hiring the largest number of seniors.
After hiring the maximum number of seniors, use the remaining budget to hire the largest number of juniors.
Write an SQL query to find the number of seniors and juniors hired under the mentioned criteria.

Return the result table in any order.

The query result format is in the following example.

 

Example 1:

Input: 
Candidates table:
+-------------+------------+--------+
| employee_id | experience | salary |
+-------------+------------+--------+
| 1           | Junior     | 10000  |
| 9           | Junior     | 10000  |
| 2           | Senior     | 20000  |
| 11          | Senior     | 20000  |
| 13          | Senior     | 50000  |
| 4           | Junior     | 40000  |
+-------------+------------+--------+
Output: 
+------------+---------------------+
| experience | accepted_candidates |
+------------+---------------------+
| Senior     | 2                   |
| Junior     | 2                   |
+------------+---------------------+
Explanation: 
We can hire 2 seniors with IDs (2, 11). Since the budget is $70000 and the sum of their salaries is $40000, we still have $30000 but they are not enough to hire the senior candidate with ID 13.
We can hire 2 juniors with IDs (1, 9). Since the remaining budget is $30000 and the sum of their salaries is $20000, we still have $10000 but they are not enough to hire the junior candidate with ID 4.
Example 2:

Input: 
Candidates table:
+-------------+------------+--------+
| employee_id | experience | salary |
+-------------+------------+--------+
| 1           | Junior     | 10000  |
| 9           | Junior     | 10000  |
| 2           | Senior     | 80000  |
| 11          | Senior     | 80000  |
| 13          | Senior     | 80000  |
| 4           | Junior     | 40000  |
+-------------+------------+--------+
Output: 
+------------+---------------------+
| experience | accepted_candidates |
+------------+---------------------+
| Senior     | 0                   |
| Junior     | 3                   |
+------------+---------------------+
Explanation: 
We cannot hire any seniors with the current budget as we need at least $80000 to hire one senior.
We can hire all three juniors with the remaining budget. 
```

### 1. Base Data: `Candidates`

This is the original dataset containing the list of potential hires, their experience level, and their required salary.

| employee_id | experience | salary |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 1 | Junior | 10000 |
| 9 | Junior | 10000 |
| 2 | Senior | 20000 |
| 11 | Senior | 20000 |
| 13 | Senior | 50000 |
| 4 | Junior | 40000 |

### 2. Base Data: `Total Budget`

This table defines the maximum total budget allowed for hiring ($70,000).

| total_budget |
| --- |
| 70000 |

### 3. Step: `Cross Join`

The script appends the `total_budget` value to every row of the candidates table using a dummy join key, making the budget accessible for row-by-row comparisons in later steps.

| employee_id | experience | salary | total_budget |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 1 | Junior | 10000 | 70000 |
| 9 | Junior | 10000 | 70000 |
| 2 | Senior | 20000 | 70000 |
| 11 | Senior | 20000 | 70000 |
| 13 | Senior | 50000 | 70000 |
| 4 | Junior | 40000 | 70000 |

### 4. Step: `Senior` (Cumulative Salary)

The query isolates the "Senior" candidates. It then uses a `List.Generate` custom function to calculate a running total (`Cumulative Salary`) of their salaries to determine how much it would cost to hire them sequentially.

| employee_id | experience | salary | total_budget | Cumulative Salary |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 2 | Senior | 20000 | 70000 | 20000 |
| 11 | Senior | 20000 | 70000 | 40000 |
| 13 | Senior | 50000 | 70000 | 90000 |

### 5. Step: `Max Senior`

The query filters the Senior table strictly for rows where the `Cumulative Salary` is less than or equal to the `total_budget`. Since 90k exceeds the 70k budget, the last candidate is filtered out. The remaining rows are grouped and counted to find the total number of accepted seniors.

| experience | accepted_candidates |
| --- | --- |
| Senior | 2 |

### 6. Step: `Senior Budget`

Running parallel, the script calculates exactly how much of the budget was actually spent on the hired Seniors. It takes the `Cumulative Salary` of the last valid hired senior.

| senior_total |
| --- |
| 40000 |

### 7. Step: `Max Junior` (Calculated Budgets & Cumulative Salary)

The script isolates the "Junior" candidates. It joins the spent `senior_total` from the previous step to calculate the remaining `junior_total` budget ($70,000 - $40,000 = $30,000). It then calculates a cumulative sum for the Junior salaries.

| employee_id | experience | salary | junior_total | Cumulative Salary |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 1 | Junior | 10000 | 30000 | 10000 |
| 9 | Junior | 10000 | 30000 | 20000 |
| 4 | Junior | 40000 | 30000 | 60000 |

### 8. Step: `Max Junior` (Hired)

Just like with the seniors, the query filters the Juniors where the `Cumulative Salary` does not exceed the remaining `junior_total` ($30,000). Since 60k exceeds 30k, the last candidate is dropped. The remaining ones are counted.

| experience | accepted_candidates |
| --- | --- |
| Junior | 2 |

### 9. Final Output: `Append`

Finally, the script combines (appends) the final counts of the hired Seniors and Juniors into one clean output table.

| experience | accepted_candidates |
| --- | --- |
| Senior | 2 |
| Junior | 2 |
